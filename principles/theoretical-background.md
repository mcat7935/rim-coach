## 1. Simon's starting point: design as state transition

The Design Enablement school (and by extension, RIM) takes Herbert Simon's definition as its starting point:

> "Everyone who devises courses of action aimed at changing existing situations into preferred ones is designing."
> — Herbert Simon, *The Sciences of the Artificial*, 1969

This definition captures the essence of design as **moving from a present state to a preferred state**. However, Simon left open the question: **who defines the "preferred state," and how?**

## 2. Krippendorff's project concept: forward motion in narrative

Klaus Krippendorff's *Semantic Turn* extends Simon by introducing the **project concept**:

**Three properties of a project**:
1. **Projects progress in narratives (物語), not blueprints** — what changes, how, by whom, when is told as a story, and the project moves through the telling.
2. **Direction can exist even amid ambiguity** — objectives, decision points, and starting conditions may all be vague, yet the direction itself attracts and motivates participants.
3. **The driving force is "openness to detail"** — designers can start a project but cannot fully control it. Activation requires creating an **open space** in which potential contributors find their own participation possibility.

Krippendorff concludes: **a project is a model of participatory design**.

### "Moving forward" — re-defined

Combining Simon and Krippendorff:

> Moving forward = **a dynamic state in which an open space, where participants can autonomously contribute, is maintained and expanded within a directional narrative.**

"Existing → preferred state" is not a pre-determined path; the "preferred state" is **co-directed** by participants through narrative.

## 3. Design Enablement: a process model for the Krippendorff concept

Design Enablement provides concrete process for the participatory model:

- **Pulling (引き出す)** — surface what the subject wants to realize through dialogue; co-create an order-made approach. Pre-supposes trust. Manifests the subject's needs / goals / vision.
- **Pushing (伝える)** — transfer formal knowledge (lectures, guidelines, etc.)
- **Demonstrating (見せる)** — transfer tacit knowledge through practice (OJT, pair design)

These cycle: **Pulling → Pushing → Demonstrating → new Pulling**.

Key distinction from Krippendorff: Krippendorff starts with "direction provided"; Pulling starts with "subject's agency." Design Enablement's uniqueness: **direction is drawn out from inside, not imposed from outside**.

## 4. The discourse problem (where RIM enters)

Krippendorff also discusses **discourse** — *organized ways of speaking, writing, and acting*. Projects run inside discourses. Two discourse-survival conditions matter most:

- **Discourse must self-justify** — failure to justify leads to atrophy. When project narrative cannot explain "why this direction," participants' open space closes and forward motion stalls.
- **Discourse must relate to other discourses** — by absorbing or confronting other discourses. When different professional discourses (business, UX, technology) coexist in isolation, the **project narrative fragments**.

**The integration problem**: Pulling and Pushing implicitly handle discourse translation/integration, but **the structural framework for that translation is not articulated** in current Design Enablement theory.

## 5. The RIM connection

**RIM addresses exactly this gap** structurally:

- The "missing structural perspective" RIM names (information defined from scattered viewpoints, not connected as a whole structure) is equivalent to *isolated discourses* in Krippendorff's language.
- RIM's response: **"a common model specialized in synthesizing"** — structurally connecting different discourses (Business / UX / Technology) to integrate the project narrative.

**Complementary positioning**:
- **RIM** = the **methodological** answer (a common model for discourse synthesis)
- **Design Enablement** = the **organizational** answer (building internal capability to operate that model continuously)

These are not exclusive but complementary. The remaining question is **how to join them**.
